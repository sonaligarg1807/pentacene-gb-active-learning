## Step 5 is: Committee wrapper for uncertainity quantification
so what is being done here and why do we do it?
what the committee does?
- train several MACE models (scratch, finetune, different random seeds) that all try to learn the same thing (energies and forces), but they differ slightly (scratch vs fine-tuned, different seeds, etc.).

- For any given structure, ask all of them: “What are the energy and forces?”

- If they all agree closely, the standard deviation (spread) of their predictions is small → the model family is confident there.

- If they disagree a lot, the standard deviation is large → the model family is uncertain there.
so the committee's job is:
"Take multiple models’ predictions and turn their disagreement into a number that you treat as “uncertainty” for each structure."

Next how active learning uses that uncertainity?
- Active learning does not try to “push the model toward certainty” in a blind, global way.

- Instead, it uses uncertainty to choose the most informative structures to label next.

You run your committee on many candidate GB structures.

For each structure, you compute the standard deviation of the committee’s predictions → uncertainty.

You select the structures with the highest uncertainty (where the models disagree most).

You label those few structures with expensive xTB.

You retrain your models on the enlarged dataset.

After this, the committee will usually become more certain specifically on those previously confusing regions of configuration space. So the goal of active learning is:
`Use uncertainty to decide where to spend labeling effort so the model becomes accurate in the places that matter most (like real grain boundaries), instead of wasting labels on already easy structures.`

what is high-uncertainity and low-uncertainity data?

-- High-uncertainty structures are those that are underrepresented or very different from your training data, so the committee models disagree there.

        These will often be GB/interface structures (weird packing, large distortions, unusual separations/rotations), especially early in the project, because your seed dataset is mostly neat dimers.

-- Low-uncertainty structures are those that are similar to what you’ve already labeled and trained on, so the committee members make similar predictions.

        These will typically look like your original dimers and “normal” stacking motifs, or regions of GBs that resemble those.

So the key is:

    “High uncertainty” is defined by model disagreement relative to the training data, not just by the physical label “GB” vs “grain interior”.

Your active learning goal is to make the model confident on GB structures by repeatedly targeting high‑uncertainty GB configurations for new xTB labels.

following is what it infers:
Energy uncertainity (std_energy) violin plot:
- Valid dimers cluster tightly around ~0.27, narrow spread (0.21–0.30).

- GB dimers have a similar median (~0.26) but a much wider spread, stretching down to ~0.17 and up to ~0.36.

- So on average, energy uncertainty looks similar, but GB dimers include both less and more uncertain cases than anything in your valid set — the committee isn't uniformly more uncertain on GB, but it's less consistent.

Force uncertainty (mean_std_force) violin plot — this is the most informative one:

  - Valid dimers: tightly packed near ~0.005, with the whole distribution below ~0.026.

  - GB dimers: clearly shifted higher, sitting around ~0.0135 median, entirely above ~0.010 minimum.

  - This is a real, visually obvious separation — GB dimers are consistently more force-uncertain than valid dimers, even though energy uncertainty looked deceptively similar. This confirms your earlier hypothesis that forces are more sensitive than energy to structural novelty.

Scatter (mean_energy vs std_energy):

  - Valid points span a wider energy range (~-2953 to ~-2941) but stay in a moderate, consistent uncertainty band.

  - GB points cluster in a narrower energy range (~-2951 to ~-2947) but show more scatter in uncertainty (both very low and the two highest points overall, up to ~0.36).

  - This suggests GB dimers are structurally more homogeneous in raw energy (similar local environments) but the committee's confidence in them varies more, i.e., some specific dimers are genuinely novel/uncertain while others aren't.

Force disagreement, not energy disagreement, is the better active-learning signal here. Energy uncertainty alone would have missed the real difference between valid and GB dimers; mean_std_force clearly separates the two populations, meaning your active learning selection criterion (Step 6c) should prioritize force-based uncertainty over energy-based uncertainty.

This is a strong, generalizable result, not a fluke of one geometry:

The committee is systematically more uncertain about forces on GB structures, regardless of misorientation axis or angle.

This means you can likely define a single, global force-uncertainty threshold (e.g., based on the valid set's maximum, ~0.026) for active learning selection, rather than needing per-system-type thresholds.

This is genuinely a nice, well-supported finding for your write-up: "committee force disagreement, not energy disagreement, robustly separates real GB structures from training-like dimers across all tested misorientations."
